So the other day the Determination came down from the City regarding 957, 963 and 967 Arapahoe.

And so, without further ado, *these* shall become *that*:

![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-02.webp)


And what *was* said determination? In short, *of course* you’re exempt from CEQA! *Of course* you can have 109 units in a sixty foot building! *Naturally* you get a 70% increase in density, because zoning laws are an outdated concept, anyhoo!



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 2](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-03.webp)

It goes on from there, another 23 pages worth, which you may read in its entirety [here](https://planning.lacity.org/pdiscaseinfo/document/ODY2NQ0/fe3b456d-e5a5-4f0e-9fa7-879f1ff43502/pdd). Spoiler alert: it doesn’t get any better. 

Of the three structures being replaced, we’ll talk first about 957 Arapahoe.



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 3](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-04.webp)

It was built by famed gambler Joseph Zemansky in the summer of 1909.



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 4](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-05.webp)



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 5](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-06.webp)

Who was this Zemansky character that could afford to build such a lavish manse?  [Famed gambler, nightclub owner, casino developer](https://gambling-history.com/10-intriguing-facts-about-gambler-businessman-joseph-zemansky/), and [the man behind Agua Caliente](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agua_Caliente,_Tijuana), that's who.  



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 6](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-07.webp)

It was the scene of [lavish weddings](https://live.staticflickr.com/65535/53199982483_a69f3868ec_o.jpg) and [much mention in the society columns](https://live.staticflickr.com/65535/53199296082_15345bfe77_o.jpg).

The house was designed by [Benjamin Cooper Corbett](https://pcad.lib.washington.edu/person/1998/), who designed a number of grand houses in Los Angeles, including the [O’Neill](https://flic.kr/p/2nEUX1S), the [Fellows](https://flic.kr/p/2k2LVke), the [Denker](https://flic.kr/p/2kcu2F5), etc.

The Zemansky house, though, now just more landfill:

![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 7](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-08.webp)

Now let’s look at its neighbor to the south, 961 Arapahoe:



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 8](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-09.webp)

This house was built by Frank A. Bowles, president of Bowles Brothers Co., the wholesale grocers. It was built in the summer-autumn of 1908.



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 9](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-10.webp)



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 10](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-11.webp)

Needless to say, as was the case with the Zemanskys next door, there were dozens of mentions in the society column, [here’s just one](https://live.staticflickr.com/65535/53199814391_a627968b70_o.jpg).   I'm extremely curious to know what remains of its Japanese tea room; oh well, to the landfill it goes.

Ah, and I should mention the architects of the Bowles house were Milwaukee Building Company, *better known as Meyer & Holler*, [who are of stupefying importance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meyer_%26_Holler).  While the developers, I'm sure, have no idea they're tearing down houses by [B. Cooper Corbett](https://windsorsquarehancockpark.com/2022/01/317-so-windsor-boulevard-windsor-square/) and Meyer & Holler, I'm sure the YIMBYs are doing a little dance, especially since they weren't able [to get this one](https://www.laconservancy.org/learn/historic-places/mendel-and-mabel-meyer-courtyard-apartments/).

And next door to that, the similarly-doomed 967 Arapahoe. It was built by physician Edward Thomas Barber in 1904, who lived there till he passed in 1926; attorney Edward Thomas Barber Jr. lived there till he passed in 1946. Unfortunately, LADBS records don’t predate 1905, and the newspapers don’t indicate who the architect was, though they do mention that Barber acted as his own contractor. In any event, it’s a great house:

![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 11](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-12.webp)



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 12](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-13.webp)

But yep, headed for the landfill, too.  

I know, some people will say “oh Nathan, but there are SO MANY of these houses in the neighborhood, what’s wrong with tearing down *just these three*?” Because we’re losing *all* of them, and fast. No-one is tearing down the beige 1980s multi-units; no-one is building on the mini-malls; I don't see anyone building on all the vacant lots everywhere. 

They NEED to tear down houses like these, for fear you are reminded of how people used to live, and how life used to be. You might argue that [Shahram Shamsian and Behrouz Bozorgnia](https://therealdeal.com/la/2022/10/28/engineering-firm-developer-mobbil-goes-green-in-koreatown/) are only in it for the money. I’m coming to believe larger forces are at work. Some people say all war is about resources; I’d argue all wars are culture wars.

But I digress. You were mentioning there were *plenty* of houses on Arapahoe? Let’s look at this cool one RIGHT ACROSS THE STREET:

![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 13](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-14.webp)

The Horace W. Skelton house, built 1905; the architect was Arthur L. Haley. Yes, the same prolific and important A. L. Haley who appears in [*Bunker Hill Los Angeles*](https://www.angelcitypress.com/products/bunk) multiple times. The little lot that once held the Skelton house at 972 Arapahoe, slated for a four-story 12-unit.

Remember yesterday, when we took a look around West 35th Street?  We could just as easily cruise Arapahoe, getting sadder and sadder.  A couple highlights, er, lowlights, though—so, six blocks south at at 1500 Arapahoe, as mentioned by Esotouric [here](https://twitter.com/esotouric/status/1621361154034638854/photo/1).

Two of the most important houses on Arapahoe, hell, in Los Angeles:

![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 14](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-15.webp)

1430 Arapahoe, at left, was built in 1885!  Now it's *that* thing.  Its neighbor 1500, an amaaaaazing structure from 1902, demolished (seen here when it became an empty lot) for a forty foot high duplex.  That's right, a three-unit became a duplex.  Good thing we're building housing!

Or consider 1218 Arapahoe, the Frederick and Ana Gros house, built in 1900, which the owners allowed to be illegally occupied by the homeless, [until it burned](https://twitter.com/reel_jamie/status/1283228178316619782).  IMAGINE MY SHOCK. Now the neighbors have *this* next door:



![957, 963 & 967 Arapahoe, Koreatown — photograph 15](~/assets/images/blog/957-963-and-967-arapahoe-16.webp)

But I'll resist the urge to go up and down Arapahoe pointing out further examples.  You get the point. And if you do, you’re apparently the only one, because nothing is stopping our beloved city from becoming...something...else.
